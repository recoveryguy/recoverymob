Compression
Therapy

Sequential pneumatic compression for lymphatic drainage. The high-return modality that most recreational athletes skip and that professional sport uses universally.

The Lymphatic Problem

The lymphatic system is responsible for clearing the metabolic waste that accumulates in muscle tissue during and after intense exercise. Inflammatory cytokines, cellular debris from muscle microtrauma, excess fluid from exercise-induced oedema — all of this must be transported through lymphatic vessels to lymph nodes for processing and eventual clearance.

The critical design constraint of the lymphatic system is that it has no pump. Unlike the cardiovascular system — which has the heart driving continuous circulation — the lymphatic system relies on muscle contractions, body movement, and gravitational pressure to move fluid. This creates a specific timing problem: the period when lymphatic clearance is most urgently needed — immediately following intense exercise, when inflammatory load in the muscle is at its peak — is precisely when the muscles responsible for driving lymphatic flow are most fatigued and least capable of providing effective contractions.

Sequential pneumatic compression solves this problem mechanically. External pressure applied in a directed, distal-to-proximal sequence substitutes for the internal muscle pumping action, driving lymphatic drainage at the rate the physiology demands rather than the rate fatigued muscles can provide.

How Sequential Compression Works

Sequential compression boots consist of multiple chambers that inflate and deflate in a programmed wave pattern — from foot to thigh — creating directional pressure that actively moves fluid upward against gravity. This is fundamentally different from static compression garments, which support venous return but do not actively drive lymphatic flow.

The wave pattern mimics and mechanically amplifies the calf muscle pump — the natural action of the calf muscles during walking that drives venous and lymphatic return from the lower limbs. After a session that has genuinely taxed the calf and leg muscles, the compression boots run this pumping action continuously, maintaining the drainage stimulus regardless of how fatigued the underlying muscles are.

What the Research Shows

Studies on sequential compression therapy in athletic populations consistently find reductions in perceived muscle soreness, improved recovery of performance markers, and reductions in blood indicators of muscle damage in the 24–48 hours following sessions that included compression versus passive recovery. The effect size is moderate and consistent — exactly the profile of a tool that contributes meaningfully across a season or training block without delivering dramatic single-session effects.

Professional sport teams use compression boots not because of one dramatic result but because the accumulation of moderate improvements across every recovery session adds up to a substantial competitive edge over the course of a season. The athlete who recovers two to three percent faster from every session accumulates significantly better training adaptation over a year than the athlete who doesn't.

Optimal Positioning in the Recovery Sequence

Compression produces its best results following cold water immersion. Cold has driven vasoconstriction and shifted the inflammatory environment toward resolution. Compression at this point actively clears the inflammatory mediators that the cold has mobilised, completing the clearance cycle that cold initiation began. The two modalities are physiologically complementary in this specific sequence — cold first, then compression, produces a more complete drainage effect than either alone or compression before cold.

Who Should Use Compression Therapy

  • Team sport athletes with 24-hour or less recovery between sessions
  • Endurance athletes managing high leg volume across training weeks
  • Anyone experiencing persistent leg heaviness or delayed soreness
  • People in physically demanding occupations accumulating lower limb fatigue
  • Athletes in intensive training blocks where daily recovery quality is critical

"The lymphatic system has no pump. After a hard session, sequential compression is that pump — and the difference in next-day soreness is consistent enough to be relied upon."
