// Photobiomodulation

Red Light
Therapy

Photobiomodulation at the therapeutic wavelength. Not wellness hype — documented mitochondrial activation with a well-characterised mechanism and growing clinical evidence for athletic recovery.

The Mechanism — What Actually Happens

Red light therapy works through a specific, well-characterised biological mechanism. Light at wavelengths between 630 and 850 nanometres is absorbed by cytochrome c oxidase — a photoreceptor enzyme in the mitochondrial respiratory chain. When cytochrome c oxidase absorbs photons at these wavelengths, the enzyme complex becomes more efficient. More electrons move through the respiratory chain. More ATP is produced. The cell has more energy available for repair and function.

Nature published an in-depth feature on the science of photobiomodulation in 2026 covering exactly this mechanism — acknowledging both its genuine scientific basis and the areas where clinical evidence continues to develop. The mechanism is not theoretical. The debate is about dose, delivery, and which clinical applications produce the most robust outcomes.

Downstream Effects Relevant to Recovery

The increase in mitochondrial ATP production from cytochrome c oxidase activation drives several downstream effects that are directly relevant to athletic recovery. Antioxidant enzyme upregulation occurs — the increased ATP production enables the cell to run its antioxidant systems more effectively, protecting against the oxidative stress that intense exercise generates. Inflammatory signalling is modulated, with prostaglandin and cytokine pathways shifting toward resolution of inflammation rather than amplification.

Nitric oxide release is another downstream effect. Nitric oxide is a potent vasodilator — its release following red light exposure improves local circulation in the tissue being irradiated, enhancing the delivery of oxygen and nutrients to areas undergoing repair. This circulatory improvement compounds the direct cellular energy production benefit.

The Evidence for Athletes

Multiple randomised controlled trials have examined red light therapy in athletic contexts. The consistent findings across well-designed trials include: reductions in delayed onset muscle soreness when red light is applied either before or after intense exercise; improved recovery of muscular force production in the 24–48 hours following eccentric exercise protocols; and reductions in blood markers of muscle damage including creatine kinase.

Pre-exercise application appears to work by preparing mitochondrial function for the energetic demands of training, reducing the oxidative damage of the session itself. Post-exercise application accelerates the repair processes that training initiated. Both approaches have clinical support, and both are used in different protocols depending on the training goal.

Device Quality — The Variable That Matters Most

Consumer red light devices vary enormously in actual irradiance — the amount of light power delivered to tissue per unit area. A device that emits red-wavelength light at insufficient irradiance produces minimal photobiomodulation regardless of its marketing claims. Clinical benefit requires reaching therapeutic irradiance levels at the tissue surface, which consumer-grade panels often fail to achieve at the distances typically used.

Commercial-grade panels used in clinical and performance settings are calibrated to deliver therapeutic doses. This gap between home devices and commercial equipment is one of the practical reasons why accessing red light therapy through a recovery facility produces more reliable outcomes than most home setups.

Integration into a Recovery Protocol

Red light therapy places no cardiovascular or thermal load on the body — it can be used on consecutive training days without meaningful cumulative fatigue. This makes it an ideal daily recovery addition. Within a structured session, it fits most naturally after compression — once inflammatory mediators have been cleared by the cold-compression sequence, red light at this point is activating cellular repair in tissue that has already had its acute inflammatory environment reduced.

"The mechanism for photobiomodulation is among the better-characterised in recovery biology. At the correct wavelength and irradiance, this is biophysics with real clinical application."
