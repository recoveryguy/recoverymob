Recovery Science: Debunking the Most Common Myths

February 2026  ·  Recovery Mob

Recovery has attracted more mythology per square metre than almost any other area of fitness. Some of these myths discourage people from using genuinely effective tools. Others push people toward ineffective practices. Here's what the evidence actually shows on the most persistent claims.

Myth 1: Ice Baths Kill Muscle Gains

This one has a kernel of real science underneath it but has been generalised far beyond what the research supports. A 2024 systematic review did find evidence that post-exercise cold water immersion may attenuate resistance training-induced hypertrophy — specifically the muscle protein synthesis signalling in the hours immediately following a strength session.

What the same research and related literature also shows: cold water immersion's attenuation effect is most pronounced when applied immediately after resistance training and is substantially reduced or absent when there is a four-to-six hour delay. Cold immersion does not globally kill muscle gains — it specifically affects the acute post-exercise hypertrophy window. For athletes training for strength and size as the primary goal, delaying cold immersion after strength sessions is appropriate. For athletes managing recovery between sessions, managing chronic inflammation, or using cold primarily for non-hypertrophy goals, the concern is overstated.

Myth 2: You Need to Stay in the Ice Bath for 10+ Minutes

Duration is not the primary determinant of cold immersion benefit. At therapeutic temperatures of 4–8°C, the primary physiological response — vasoconstriction, norepinephrine release, inflammatory marker reduction — occurs within the first two to three minutes. Extending to ten minutes at this temperature adds diminishing returns while increasing the cold stress burden.

The ten-plus minute protocol comes from cold immersion at warmer temperatures — 12–15°C — where longer duration compensates for reduced intensity. At proper therapeutic temperatures, two to four minutes is a complete dose. More is not better.

Myth 3: Sauna is Dangerous for Athletes

Sauna is safe for healthy adults at normal frequencies and durations. The Finnish cohort data on sauna and cardiovascular health shows the opposite of danger — regular sauna use is associated with substantially reduced cardiovascular mortality risk. Sauna does place cardiovascular demand on the body — heart rate increases, blood pressure changes — but this is the mechanism of the benefit, not a risk for people without pre-existing cardiovascular conditions.

The specific concern about sauna and athletes usually involves dehydration. This is real but manageable: drink adequately before and after sauna sessions, avoid sauna when already significantly dehydrated from training, and limit alcohol consumption in combination with sauna. With these basic precautions, sauna is not dangerous for healthy athletes and is actively beneficial at regular frequencies.

Myth 4: Red Light Therapy is Pseudoscience

Red light therapy has been genuinely oversold by some wellness marketers, which has contributed to a sceptical backlash that sometimes goes too far. The mechanism — cytochrome c oxidase absorption of red and near-infrared photons increasing mitochondrial ATP production — is documented and reproducible. The clinical evidence for athletic recovery applications, while not uniformly strong, includes multiple RCTs showing meaningful reductions in delayed onset muscle soreness and improved recovery of muscular force production.

The legitimate criticism is not of the mechanism but of the devices: most consumer red light products deliver insufficient irradiance to produce meaningful photobiomodulation at typical use distances. The science is real; many of the products marketed around it don't actually deliver the dose required to produce the effects the science documents.

Myth 5: Active Recovery is Always Better Than Passive Rest

Active recovery — light movement, low-intensity exercise — is generally superior to complete passive rest for metabolic waste clearance and maintaining circulation in recovering tissue. But "active recovery" has been used to justify continuing to train through fatigue on the grounds that movement is always better than rest.

Complete rest is appropriate when the nervous system, not the muscles, is the primary recovery target. After extremely high-intensity training blocks, competition, or periods of accumulated stress, genuine rest — not light movement but actual downtime — is necessary for central nervous system recovery that active recovery does not provide. Distinguishing between muscular and neural fatigue, and matching the recovery approach to the type of fatigue present, is more sophisticated than the blanket "keep moving" advice that active recovery is sometimes used to justify.

Myth 6: Recovery Modalities Are Only for Elite Athletes

This myth is both practically incorrect and economically motivated. Access to recovery facilities was historically limited to professional sport environments — which created the perception that these tools are for elite athletes. Access has democratised significantly. Facilities like Primal Recovery Centre in Moorabbin offer the full stack — ice baths, sauna, compression, red light, heated magnesium spa — at day pass pricing that makes them accessible to recreational athletes.

The physiology doesn't care about your level. Cold immersion produces the same vasoconstriction and norepinephrine release in a recreational Muay Thai practitioner as in a professional AFL player. The recovery benefit is proportionate to the training load — which means it's most valuable for the people training hard enough to genuinely need it, regardless of whether they're being paid for it.
